Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi (1165–1240 CE), revered in the Sufi tradition as Al-Shaykh al-Akbar ("The Greatest Master") and known in the West as Doctor Maximus, was a legendary Andalusian mystic, philosopher, and poet. He is the central figure of classical Islamic mysticism, famous for formulating the metaphysical system of Wahdat al-Wujud ("The Unity of Being"). Ibn Arabi mapped the cosmos as a series of divine self-disclosures, bridging rational metaphysics with mystical gnosis (irfan) to produce one of the most sophisticated ontologies in intellectual history.

Biography: The Journey of the Spirit

Ibn Arabi was born in Murcia, Spain, during the height of Islamic Andalusian culture. In his early teens, he experienced a mystical vision during a illness, which directed him to the Sufi path. His father, a government official, was a friend of the philosopher Ibn Rushd. Hearing of the young Ibn Arabi's spiritual insight, Ibn Rushd requested a meeting in Cordoba. In his writings, Ibn Arabi recounts this famous meeting: the aging Peripatetic philosopher asked the young mystic if rational investigation and mystical experience arrived at the same truth. Ibn Arabi replied, "Yes and no. Between the 'yes' and the 'no' souls fly from their matter." Ibn Rushd reportedly wept, realizing the depth of the youth's spiritual attainment.

Following a divine command received in a dream, Ibn Arabi left Spain in 1200 CE, embarking on a lifelong journey through North Africa, Egypt, Anatolia, and Syria. He performed the pilgrimage to Mecca, where he spent several years and began writing his monumental encyclopedia, The Meccan Revelations. He was patronized by Seljuk rulers in Anatolia and finally settled in Damascus, where he taught and composed his final masterpieces until his death in 1240 CE.

Wahdat al-Wujud: The Unity of Being

The core of Ibn Arabi's metaphysics is the doctrine of **Wahdat al-Wujud**. The term itself was not used by Ibn Arabi, but was coined by his disciples to summarize his complex ontology.

Unlike pantheism, which asserts that the universe *is* God, Ibn Arabi's system maintains a distinction between the Creator and the creation, while asserting that only God has absolute, independent **existence** (Wujud): 1. **The Divine Essence (al-Dhat):** In His innermost reality, God is the absolute, unmanifested Transcendent One, completely beyond human comprehension. 2. **The Divine Names and Attributes:** To be known, God manifests Himself through His Names (the Merciful, the Creator, the All-Knowing). 3. **The Immutable Archetypes (al-A'yan al-Thabitah):** All created things exist eternally in God's mind as potentials or ideas. 4. **The Physical Cosmos:** Created things do not possess existence in themselves. Instead, God projects His light onto the archetypes, bringing them into temporal manifestation. The universe is like a mirror reflecting the divine names, or a shadow cast by the divine light. Everything we see is a self-manifestation (tajalli) of the Single Reality. Ibn Arabi cited verses like Surah Al-Baqarah (2:115): "So wherever you turn, there is the Face of Allah," and Surah Al-Hadid (57:4): "He is with you wherever you are."

The Perfect Man (al-Insan al-Kamil)

In Ibn Arabi's cosmology, the human being occupies a unique and exalted position. He introduced the concept of the **Perfect Man** (al-Insān al-Kāmil) to explain the purpose of creation.

While the animals and elements reflect only individual divine names (e.g., a lion reflects majesty, a flower reflects beauty), the human being was created in the divine image, containing all the divine names in potential. The Perfect Man is the individual who has fully realized this potential. He acts as a microcosm of the universe and a polished mirror in whom God contemplates His own names and attributes. He is the channel through whom divine grace and existence flow to the rest of creation, serving as the bridge (barzakh) between the Creator and the cosmos.

Major Works

  • Al-Futuhat al-Makkiyya (The Meccan Revelations): A massive 37-volume encyclopedia of mystical theology, cosmology, ethics, and Sharia, which Ibn Arabi claimed was revealed to him by a spiritual entity in Mecca.
  • Fusus al-Hikam (The Bezels of Wisdom): His final and most famous work, consisting of 27 chapters, each dedicated to the spiritual wisdom of a specific prophet in the Quran (from Adam to Muhammad).
  • Tarjuman al-Ashwaq (The Interpreter of Desires): A beautiful collection of mystical love poetry, representing the soul's longing for the Divine.

Legacy and Polemics

Ibn Arabi's thought shaped the subsequent history of Islamic mysticism, establishing the vocabulary of Sufi metaphysics throughout Persia, India, and the Ottoman Empire. However, his bold assertions also drew intense criticism. The Athari reformer Ibn Taymiyyah and later scholars accused him of pantheism and heresy, leading to centuries of debate (known as the *Akbarian controversy*) over the orthodoxy of his writings. Today, he is studied globally as a master of metaphysics and inter-religious dialogue.

"He is the mirror in which you see yourself, and you are the mirror in which He contemplates His Names and manifests His attributes. The universe is a mirror in which the Divine Names are reflected, but the mirror was unpolished until the creation of Adam, who was the very polish of that mirror. The human being is the spirit of the world, and the world is the body that is animated by him."
— Ibn Arabi, The Bezels of Wisdom (Fusus al-Hikam)
