Abu Hamid al-Ghazali (1058–1111 CE), known in the West as Algazel and revered in the Islamic tradition as Hujjat al-Islam ("The Proof of Islam"), was a Persian theologian, jurist, logician, and mystic. He is widely considered one of the most influential thinkers in Islamic history, famous for his intellectual defense of Sunni orthodoxy, his critique of Greek-influenced rationalism (Falsafa), and his ultimate reconciliation of Islamic law (Sharia) with Sufi mysticism.

Biography and Spiritual Crisis

Al-Ghazali was born in Tus, in the Khorasan province of Persia. He received a traditional Islamic education, studying under the preeminent Ash'arite theologian Al-Juwanyi. Recognizing his immense intellectual talent, the Seljuk vizier Nizam al-Mulk appointed Al-Ghazali as the head professor of the prestigious Nizamiyyah Academy in Baghdad in 1091 CE. At the age of 34, he reached the absolute pinnacle of academic, legal, and political influence in the Islamic world.

However, in 1095 CE, Al-Ghazali suffered a profound spiritual and psychological crisis. He began to doubt the foundations of his own knowledge and motivations, realizing that his teaching was driven by a desire for fame and status rather than sincere devotion to God. The crisis manifested physically; he lost the ability to speak, preventing him from lecturing. He resigned from his post, distributed his wealth, and left Baghdad under the guise of going on a pilgrimage to Mecca.

For the next eleven years, Al-Ghazali lived as a wandering ascetic, traveling through Damascus, Jerusalem, Hebron, and Mecca. He practiced spiritual retreat, fasting, and contemplation under the guidance of Sufi masters. During this period of isolation, he experienced the mystical "tasting" (dhawq) of divine reality, resolving his skepticism and finding certainty not in rational proofs, but in a divine light cast into his heart. He recounts this journey in his famous spiritual autobiography, Al-Munqidh min al-Dalal (Deliverance from Error).

The Critique of Peripateticism (Falsafa)

Before criticizing the philosophers, Al-Ghazali spent years studying their works until he understood them better than they did themselves. He wrote Maqasid al-Falasifah (The Intentions of the Philosophers), a highly objective summary of Avicennian metaphysics and logic. Once he had established his expertise, he published his devastating counter-offensive: Tahafut al-Falasifah (The Incoherence of the Philosophers).

Al-Ghazali did not reject logic or mathematics, which he deemed neutral tools. Instead, his critique targeted their metaphysics. He argued that the philosophers (principally Al-Farabi and Ibn Sina) had failed to prove their metaphysical assertions using their own standards of logic. He identified twenty distinct errors in their system. While he classified seventeen of these as heterodox innovations (bid'ah), he declared three to be outright disbelief (kufr) because they directly contradicted scriptural revelation:

  1. The Eternity of the World: The philosophers argued that the universe emanated eternally from God. Al-Ghazali asserted the orthodox view of creation out of nothing (creatio ex nihilo) at a specific point in time by God's absolute, unconstrained will.
  2. God's Knowledge of Universals Only: Ibn Sina posited that God, being absolute and unchanging, only knows the universe in a universal, non-temporal way, rather than knowing specific, changing particulars (such as an individual leaf falling). Al-Ghazali argued this denies God's omniscience, citing verses like Surah Saba (34:3): "Not absent from Him is an atom's weight within the heavens or within the earth."
  3. The Denial of Physical Resurrection: The philosophers believed that after death, only the immaterial soul survives, and rewards or punishments in the afterlife are purely spiritual. Al-Ghazali defended the literal physical resurrection of the body, asserting that God has the power to recreate physical forms, as described in the Quran.

The Philosophy of Occasionalism (Causality)

To preserve the absolute sovereignty and omnipotence of God, Al-Ghazali formulated a philosophical critique of natural causality, a position known in Western philosophy as Occasionalism.

He argued that the connection between what we habitually perceive as a "cause" (e.g., fire) and an "effect" (e.g., burning cotton) is not a relationship of logical necessity. Fire does not possess an inherent, independent power to burn. Rather, the burning of the cotton is created directly by God's active will at the exact moment ("occasion") the fire touches the cotton. God is the only true efficient cause in the universe. What humans perceive as "laws of nature" are simply the habitual customs ('adah) of God's action, which He can suspend at any time to perform miracles (such as Prophet Abraham surviving the furnace, linked to Surah Al-Anfal (8:17): "And you threw not when you threw, but it was Allah who threw").

The Integration of Sufism and Orthodox Law

Al-Ghazali's greatest positive contribution was the integration of Sufism (mystical spirituality) into mainstream Sunni orthodoxy. In his monumental 40-volume work, Ihya' Ulum al-Din (The Revival of the Religious Sciences), he revitalized the dry, formalist legalism of the jurists by showing that the outer rituals of Islamic law (prayer, fasting, charity) are meaningless without their inner spiritual realities (sincerity, fear of God, love). He successfully demonstrated that Sufi spiritual purification is the internal dimension of the Sharia, saving mysticism from heresy and law from stagnation.

Major Works

  • Ihya' Ulum al-Din (The Revival of the Religious Sciences): His masterwork, bridging jurisprudence, theology, and Sufism to revive the spiritual life of the community.
  • Tahafut al-Falasifah (The Incoherence of the Philosophers): The definitive intellectual critique of Aristotelian metaphysics in the Islamic tradition.
  • Al-Munqidh min al-Dalal (Deliverance from Error): A spiritual autobiography describing his philosophical skepticism, academic crisis, and discovery of Sufism.
  • Al-Mustasfa min 'Ilm al-Usul (The Clarified Theory of Islamic Jurisprudence): A highly influential work on the methodology of Islamic law, where he introduced Aristotelian logic as a preliminary science for all religious studies.

Legacy and Impact

Al-Ghazali's synthesis profoundly shaped the course of Islamic civilization. His critique of Falsafa shifted the intellectual focus of the Sunni world from Peripatetic philosophy toward theological Kalam and Sufi metaphysics. In Europe, his works were translated into Latin in the 12th century. His critique of causality influenced Christian thinkers like Nicolas of Autrecourt and Malebranche, and anticipated the modern skepticism of David Hume by seven centuries. He remains a model of the integration of intellect, law, and spiritual experience.

Primary Source Excerpts

"I knew for sure that the Sufis are those who uniquely follow the way of God Most High, their lives are the most beautiful, their way is the straightest, and their character is the purest. Even if the intellect of the rationalists, the wisdom of the philosophers, and the knowledge of the scholars were combined to improve their way and character, they could not find a way to do so. For all their movements and stillness, both outer and inner, are drawn from the niche of prophetic revelation, and there is no light on the face of the earth brighter than the light of prophecy."
— Al-Ghazali, Deliverance from Error (Al-Munqidh min al-Dalal)
"The connection between what is habitually believed to be a cause and what is believed to be an effect is not necessary according to us. But any two things... which are not identical, and where the affirmation of one does not imply the affirmation of the other, do not have a necessary connection. Their connection is due to the prior decree of God, who creates them side by side, not because one has an inherent power in itself to cause the other."
— Al-Ghazali, The Incoherence of the Philosophers (Tahafut al-Falasifah)
