Wahdat al-Wujud (Arabic: وحدة الوجود, literally "the Unity of Existence" or "Unity of Being") is a major school of Sufi metaphysical ontology that asserts that all existence is ultimately a single, unified Reality (God), and that all created things are merely reflections, shadows, or self-manifestations (tajalli) of that one divine source. Formulated by the followers of the Andalusian mystic Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi, this doctrine represents one of the most sophisticated metaphysical frameworks in the history of mysticism, dominating classical Islamic spirituality for centuries.

Origin and Systematic Formulation

While the spiritual foundations of the doctrine are found in the Quran and early Sufi utterances, it was Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi (1165–1240 CE) who provided its metaphysical structure. Ibn Arabi himself did not use the exact phrase "Wahdat al-Wujud" in his writings. The term was popularized and developed into a systematic school of philosophy by his primary disciple, **Sadr al-Din al-Qunawi** (died 1274 CE) of Konya, Turkey. Qunawi synthesized Ibn Arabi's mystical insights with Peripatetic philosophical terminology, making the school accessible to subsequent generations of philosophers and theologians.

Metaphysical Structure of Unity

To prevent the accusation of pantheism (which collapses the distinction between God and the physical universe), the school of Wahdat al-Wujud maintains a strict distinction between the Divine Essence and His manifestations, organizing reality into five hierarchical levels of self-disclosure (known as the **Five Divine Presences**):

  1. The Divine Essence (al-Dhat): The absolute, unmanifested transcendent reality. In this state, God is completely hidden and beyond all relations, attributes, or names. This is the level of absolute unity (Ahadiyyah).
  2. The Divine Names and Attributes: The level where God manifests Himself to Himself, realizing His names (the Merciful, the Creator, the King). This is the level of qualified unity (Wahidiyyah).
  3. The Angelic World (al-Jabarut): The world of spiritual, non-material entities and angelic intellects.
  4. The Imaginal World (al-Malakut): An intermediate world of subtle forms, where spiritual realities take on shapes and physical shapes have spiritual meanings.
  5. The Physical World (al-Nasut): The material universe, which is the densest and most passive manifestation of the divine names.

According to this ontology, physical things do not possess existence (Wujud) in their own right. Existence is a single light that belongs solely to God. Created things are "possible entities" whose reality is like a mirror reflecting the divine attributes. The universe is compared to a shadow cast by the divine light, or a wave that appears on the surface of the absolute ocean of existence.

Scriptural Anchors

Practitioners of Wahdat al-Wujud argue that their ontology is the inner meaning of the Quranic testimony of faith: La ilaha illa Allah ("There is no deity but God"), which they interpret metaphysically as: "There is no existence but the Divine Existence." They ground this in several Quranic verses:

  • Surah Al-Baqarah (2:115): "And to Allah belongs the east and the west. So wherever you turn, there is the Face of Allah."
  • Surah Al-Hadid (57:3): "He is the First and the Last, the Ascendant and the Intimate, and He is, of all things, Knowing."
  • Surah Al-Anfal (8:17): "And you threw not when you threw, but it was Allah who threw."

The Akbarian Controversy

The doctrine of Wahdat al-Wujud sparked intense intellectual debates across the Islamic world: * **The Critics:** Led by **Ibn Taymiyyah**, critics argued that the doctrine was a form of heretical pantheism or incarnationism (Hulul). They claimed that by equating creation with the Creator, the school destroyed the moral distinction between good and evil, and undermined the necessity of religious law (Sharia). * **The Defenders:** Scholars like Jami, Abd al-Karim al-Jili, and Qutb al-Din al-Shirazi defended the school, arguing that it preserves absolute monotheism (Tawhid) in its purest form, and that the distinction between Creator and creation is maintained at the level of manifestation, upholding Sharia. * **Unity of Witness (Wahdat al-Shuhud):** In 17th-century India, the reformer **Ahmad Sirhindi** proposed an alternative doctrine called *Wahdat al-Shuhud* ("Unity of Witness"), arguing that the feeling of unity with God is a subjective mystical state of the observer, rather than an objective ontological reality of the universe.

Major Works

  • Fusus al-Hikam (The Bezels of Wisdom): Ibn Arabi's primary text outlining the metaphysical principles of the school.
  • Miftah al-Ghayb (The Key to the Unseen World): Sadr al-Din al-Qunawi's systematic philosophical exposition of the doctrine.
  • Lama'at (Divine Flashes): Fakhr al-Din al-Iraqi's beautiful, poetic commentary on the metaphysics of love and existence.

Primary Source Excerpt

"He is the Essence of all that is manifest, and He is the Hidden Essence of all that is unmanifest. The creation is nothing but His outer garment, and His reality is the inner spirit of all that exists. The servant is the Lord, and the Lord is the servant; would that I knew who is the one obligated to pray! If you say the servant, he is dead; if you say the Lord, how can He be obligated? Realize that you are a mirror reflecting His Names, and your existence is a loan from His light."
— Ibn Arabi, The Bezels of Wisdom (Fusus al-Hikam)
