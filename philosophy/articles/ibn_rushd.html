Ibn Rushd (1126–1198 CE), known to the Western world as Averroes and celebrated in European history as "The Commentator", was a prominent Andalusian Arab polymath, jurist, physician, and philosopher. Working at the western frontier of the Islamic world in Cordoba, he spent his life defending the compatibility of Islamic law (Sharia) and rational philosophy (Falsafa), and wrote the most detailed commentaries on Aristotle produced in the medieval era. His works sparked the rise of secular philosophy in Western Europe, serving as a catalyst for the European Renaissance.

Biography of a Judge-Philosopher

Ibn Rushd was born in Cordoba, Spain, into a prestigious family of legal scholars. His grandfather was the chief judge (Qadi) of Cordoba, a position Ibn Rushd himself would later occupy. He received a rigorous education in Islamic law, theology, literature, medicine, and philosophy. Ibn Rushd was introduced to the Almohad Caliph Abu Yaqub Yusuf by the philosopher Ibn Tufail. The Caliph, an avid intellectual, complained that Aristotle's texts were obscure and difficult to understand, and commissioned Ibn Rushd to write detailed commentaries explaining them.

For the next three decades, Ibn Rushd served as a judge in Seville and Cordoba, and as the personal physician to the Caliph, while working tirelessly on his philosophical treatises. However, toward the end of his life, as the Almohad Caliphate faced military pressure from Christian kingdoms, the Caliph sought to appease orthodox theologians. Ibn Rushd was declared a heretic, his philosophical books were burned, and he was exiled to Lucena, a small town near Cordoba. He was rehabilitated shortly before his death and died in Marrakech, Morocco, in 1198 CE.

The Decisive Treatise (Fasl al-Maqal)

Ibn Rushd's most famous legal-philosophical work is Fasl al-Maqal (The Decisive Treatise), written in the form of a formal legal opinion (Fatwa). In it, he addresses a fundamental question: Is the study of philosophy and logic permissible, prohibited, or obligatory under Islamic law?

His legal argument proceeds as follows:

  1. Definition of Philosophy: Philosophy is defined as the study of existing beings and reflection upon them as proof of the Creator. The better we understand the design of the universe, the better we understand its Designer.
  2. The Scriptural Mandate: The Quran repeatedly commands believers to use their intellect and reflect upon creation, citing verses like Surah Al-Hashr (59:2): "Reflect, O people of vision," and Surah Ali 'Imran (3:191): "And they reflect on the creation of the heavens and the earth." Since reflection must follow logical rules to avoid error, the study of logic and philosophy is a religious command.
  3. Legal Status: Ibn Rushd concludes that the study of philosophy is **obligatory** (wajib) for those who possess the intellectual capacity to study it, and recommended for others.
  4. Reconciling Conflicts: Ibn Rushd famously declares: "Truth cannot contradict truth." Since philosophy is true and scriptural revelation is true, they cannot conflict. If a literal reading of scripture appears to contradict a logical, demonstrative proof, it is a sign that the scripture must not be read literally. In such cases, the jurist must interpret the scripture allegorically (ta'wil) using the rules of language.

The Three Classes of Minds

To explain why religious texts contain both literal and allegorical meanings, Ibn Rushd divided human intellects into three distinct classes, arguing that God addressed each class according to its capacity:

  • The Demonstrative Class: Philosophers who seek truth through rigorous, deductive logical proofs. They are capable of understanding abstract metaphysical truths directly.
  • The Dialectical Class: Theologians (Kalam) who are moved by dialectical arguments, debate, and rationalizing dogma. Ibn Rushd criticized them for causing division and confusion by raising doubts without offering absolute proofs.
  • The Rhetorical Class: The vast majority of humanity, who are moved by stories, vivid imagery, and moral symbols. They require the literal, visual descriptions of scripture (like a physical paradise and hell) to lead moral lives.

Ibn Rushd warned that it is a grave sin to share allegorical interpretations with the rhetorical class, as it destroys their faith. Religious scriptures are designed with an outer literal meaning for the masses and an inner allegorical meaning for the philosophers.

The Response to Al-Ghazali (Tahafut al-Tahafut)

To rescue rationalism in the Islamic world, Ibn Rushd wrote a detailed, line-by-line refutation of Al-Ghazali's Incoherence of the Philosophers, titled Tahafut al-Tahafut (The Incoherence of the Incoherence).

He defended the philosophers against the charge of disbelief, arguing that Al-Ghazali's arguments were based on dialectical tricks rather than demonstrative logic. Most notably, Ibn Rushd defended **causality** against Al-Ghazali's occasionalism. He argued that if we deny that things have inherent causal properties (e.g., that fire has a natural property to burn), then we deny that things have a stable nature. If things have no stable nature, they cannot be defined or known. Consequently, rejecting causality makes science, logic, and rational knowledge impossible, reducing the universe to arbitrary chaos.

Averroism and European Impact

While Ibn Rushd's philosophy was largely rejected in the post-classical Sunni world in favor of theology and mysticism, his writings had a explosive impact on Western Europe. His commentaries on Aristotle were translated into Latin by scholars like Michael Scot and widely read in medieval universities. This gave rise to **Latin Averroism**, a radical intellectual movement led by figures like Siger of Brabant, who advocated for the independence of philosophy from theology.

Latin Averroists were accused of holding the "Double Truth Theory" (that something could be true in philosophy but false in theology), a concept Ibn Rushd himself never held. His work forced Christian scholars, most notably **Thomas Aquinas**, to engage with Aristotelian philosophy. Aquinas wrote extensively to combat Averroes' theory of the "unity of the intellect" (the idea that all humans share a single, cosmic active intellect), yet Aquinas referred to Averroes simply as "The Commentator" and adopted his rationalist methodology to compile Christian theology.

Major Works

  • Tahafut al-Tahafut (The Incoherence of the Incoherence): His systematic defense of Falsafa and causality against Al-Ghazali.
  • Fasl al-Maqal (The Decisive Treatise): A legal defense of logic and philosophy as commanded by Islamic law.
  • Aristotle Commentaries (Short, Middle, and Long): Detailed analyses of Aristotle's works on metaphysics, physics, logic, and the soul.
  • Kitab al-Kashf (The Exposition of the Methods of Proof): A critique of the theological sects (Ash'arites, Mu'tazilites) and an exposition of his own theological views.

— Ibn Rushd, The Incoherence of the Incoherence (Tahafut al-Tahafut)
