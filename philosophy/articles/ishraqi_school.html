Illuminationist Philosophy (Arabic: حكمت الإشراق, Hikmat al-Ishraq) is a major school of Islamic philosophy founded by the Persian thinker Shihab al-Din al-Suhrawardi in the late 12th century CE. It emerged as a fundamental critique of the dominant rationalist Aristotelianism (Falsafa) of Ibn Sina, proposing instead an ontology based on **Light** (Nur) and an epistemology that balances rigorous logical training with intuitive, mystical illumination. The school had a profound and lasting impact on Islamic thought in Persia, India, and Turkey.

The Meaning of Ishraq

The Arabic word Ishraq means "rising" or "illumination," referring to the rising of the sun in the East (Mashriq). In Suhrawardi's philosophy, Ishraq has a double meaning: * **Geographical Wisdom:** It refers to the "Oriental Wisdom" of the ancient East, which Suhrawardi believed was possessed by the ancient Persians (Zoroastrians) and Greeks (Plato, Hermes) but was lost in Aristotelian Peripateticism. * **Metaphysical Illumination:** It refers to the spiritual light that rises in the purified soul of the philosopher, providing direct, unmediated knowledge of reality.

Core Concepts of Illuminationism

1. Metaphysics of Light (Nur)

Ishraqi philosophy replaces the Aristotelian ontology of "being and substance" with a metaphysics of **Light** (Nur). * **Reality as Light:** Suhrawardi argued that all things in the universe differ only in their degree of light and darkness. * **The Light of Lights (Nur al-Anwar):** God is the Light of Lights, the supreme, self-luminous source of all existence. His light overflows eternally, emanating a hierarchy of lesser, incorporeal lights (angels, human souls) and physical bodies, which are simply dark barriers (barzakh) that block light. * **Quranic Verse of Light:** This metaphysics draws direct scriptural inspiration from the Verse of Light (Surah An-Nur 24:35): "Allah is the Light of the heavens and the earth. The example of His light is like a niche within which is a lamp..."

2. Knowledge by Presence (al-Ilm al-Huduri)

While the Peripatetic philosophers relied on conceptual, discursive reasoning (logical definitions and syllogisms) to acquire knowledge, the Illuminationists argued that this conceptual knowledge is always indirect and incomplete. * They introduced **Knowledge by Presence** (al-Ilm al-Huduri): direct, immediate, intuitive awareness, identical to the way the soul knows its own existence. * The philosopher must purify their soul through asceticism and meditation. Once the soul is clean, it becomes a mirror that receives direct, illuminated visions from the celestial angelic lights, providing certainty beyond rational doubt.

3. Syncretism of East and West

Suhrawardi sought to reconstruct a "perennial philosophy" that unified the ancient wisdom of both East and West. He synthesized: * The **Platonic Forms** (viewing them as angelic lights). * The **Zoroastrian Symbolism** of light and darkness (de-mythologizing them and placing them within Islamic monotheism). * The **Sufi Mystical States** of ecstasy and annihilation.

Historical Influence and the School of Isfahan

Following Suhrawardi's execution, his works were commented upon by scholars like Shahrazuri and Qutb al-Din al-Shirazi, who preserved his ideas. During the 17th-century Safavid cultural renaissance, Ishraqi philosophy became a core component of the **School of Isfahan**, serving as the bridge to Mulla Sadra's Transcendent Theosophy. It remains a vibrant tradition in contemporary Shia philosophy.

Major Works

  • Hikmat al-Ishraq (The Philosophy of Illumination): Suhrawardi's foundational masterwork.
  • Hayakil al-Nur (The Temples of Light): A concise summary of the soul's nature and journey.
  • The Red Intellect (Aql-e Surkh): A highly symbolic Persian mystical treatise.

Primary Source Excerpt

"True wisdom is not obtained by conceptual proof and discursive reasoning alone, but by direct tasting (dhawq) and spiritual vision. He who has not experienced the illumination of the Light of Lights remains in the darkness of concepts. The Light of Lights is that which is self-luminous and makes all other things manifest, too clear to require definition."
— Suhrawardi, The Philosophy of Illumination (Hikmat al-Ishraq)
