Ibn Sina (980–1037 CE), known to the Western world as Avicenna and designated in the Islamic tradition as Al-Shaykh al-Ra'is ("The Leader among Wise Men"), was a Persian polymath who stands as the most influential philosopher-scientist of the Islamic Golden Age. He synthesized Aristotelian logic, Neoplatonic metaphysics, and Islamic theology into a unified rationalist framework known as Avicennian Metaphysics, which dominated Islamic intellectual thought for centuries and laid the foundation for medieval European scholasticism.

Biography of a Prodigy

Ibn Sina was born in Afshana, a village near Bukhara (in modern-day Uzbekistan). A child prodigy, he had memorized the entire Quran by the age of ten. He mastered Indian arithmetic, Islamic jurisprudence, geometry, and astronomy in his early teens. By sixteen, he turned his attention to medicine, declaring it "not difficult," and was treating patients for free. He famously read Aristotle's Metaphysics forty times until he memorized it, but could not understand its purpose until he stumbled upon Al-Farabi's brief commentary on it in a bookstall.

At age eighteen, Ibn Sina successfully cured the Samanid Emir of Bukhara of a severe illness. In gratitude, the Emir granted him access to the royal library, the Siwan al-Hikmah, which contained rare manuscripts on every science. This allowed Ibn Sina to absorb the entirety of ancient and contemporary knowledge. Following the collapse of the Samanid dynasty, Ibn Sina led a turbulent life, wandering through various Persian courts (Rey, Hamadan, Isfahan) as a vizier, court physician, and advisor, writing his monumental works during times of peace and even while imprisoned.

The Proof of the Truthful (Burhan al-Siddiqin)

Ibn Sina formulated a revolutionary cosmological argument for the existence of God, which he named the "Proof of the Truthful". Unlike previous arguments that relied on physical motion (like Aristotle's Prime Mover) or creation in time (like the Kalam theologians), Ibn Sina's proof is purely metaphysical, relying solely on the analysis of existence itself.

His argument proceeds in four main logical steps:

  1. The Division of Beings: Everything that exists can be classified into two categories:
    • Contingent Existents (Mumkin al-Wujud): Things that can either exist or not exist. In themselves, they are neutral to existence. To exist, they require an external cause to tip the scale from non-existence to existence. Everything we see in the physical universe is contingent.
    • The Necessary Existent (Wajib al-Wujud): An entity whose non-existence is impossible. Its essence is identical to its existence; it does not require any external cause to exist.
  2. The Need for a Cause: Any contingent thing must have an external cause for its existence.
  3. The Rejection of Infinite Regress: If we try to explain a contingent thing by pointing to another contingent cause, which in turn points to another, we create a causal chain. If this chain is infinite and contains only contingent things, the entire chain itself remains contingent and requires an external cause to exist. Therefore, an infinite regress or circular loop of contingent causes is logically impossible.
  4. Conclusion: To explain the existence of contingent things, there must be a primary cause that is not contingent, but exists by its own nature. This is the **Necessary Existent**, which possesses absolute unity, is immaterial, has no parts, and is identical to God. He linked this metaphysical absolute to the tranquil soul in Surah Al-Fajr (89:27).

The Floating Man Thought Experiment (The Soul)

To prove the immateriality, independence, and self-awareness of the human soul (nafs), Ibn Sina proposed his famous "Floating Man" thought experiment, anticipating Rene Descartes' Cogito, ergo sum by six hundred years.

He asks the reader to imagine a human being created in mid-air all at once, fully mature and with a healthy intellect. This person's eyes are covered so they cannot see, their ears are blocked so they cannot hear, and they are floating in a void or airless space with their limbs splayed out so they do not touch each other. The person has no sensory inputs whatsoever—no sight, no sound, no touch, and no memory of physical experience.

Ibn Sina then asks: "Would this person be aware of their own existence?"

He answers that the floating man would undoubtedly affirm: "I exist." Even though he cannot affirm the existence of his eyes, his hands, his heart, or the physical world, he cannot doubt his own self-awareness. Since he can conceive of his soul existing without his body, the soul must be a distinct, non-physical substance that is independent of the body. The body is merely an instrument that the soul uses during its earthly life.

Epistemology, Prophetology, and the Active Intellect

In his epistemology, Ibn Sina divided the human intellect into a hierarchy of developmental stages, ranging from the potential to receive knowledge (Material Intellect) to the active possession of abstract concepts (Acquired Intellect). He posited that the ultimate source of all abstract knowledge is the **Active Intellect** (al-Aql al-Fa'al), a cosmic, non-material intelligence that emanates from God and acts as the "giver of forms" to human minds.

This framework allowed Ibn Sina to explain **prophecy** in rational terms. A prophet is a human being endowed with an exceptionally refined, "sacred intellect" (al-aql al-qudsi). While ordinary humans must study and use syllogisms to acquire knowledge step-by-step, the prophet's intellect connects instantaneously with the Active Intellect, receiving the totality of universal truths all at once. The prophet's creative imagination then translates these abstract truths into visual symbols and moral laws (scripture) that can be understood by ordinary people.

Scientific and Medical Legacy

Ibn Sina was as much a scientist and physician as he was a philosopher. He wrote The Canon of Medicine (al-Qanun fi al-Tibb), a massive five-volume medical encyclopedia. It systematized Greek, Roman, Persian, and Indian medical knowledge with his own clinical observations. The Canon introduced revolutionary medical concepts:

  • The contagious nature of tuberculosis and other infectious diseases.
  • The concept of quarantine to limit the spread of infections.
  • Scientific testing of drugs, establishing the foundations of clinical pharmacology.
  • Recognizing the influence of psychological states (stress, sorrow) on physical health, pioneering psychosomatic medicine.

His scientific masterwork, The Book of Healing (Kitab al-Shifa), is not a book on medicine, but a monumental encyclopedia of logic, physics, mathematics, geometry, meteorology, and metaphysics, designed to "heal" the soul of ignorance.

Major Works

  • Kitab al-Shifa (The Book of Healing): His grand philosophical and scientific encyclopedia.
  • Al-Qanun fi al-Tibb (The Canon of Medicine): The standard textbook of medicine in Europe and the Islamic world until the 17th century.
  • Kitab al-Najat (The Book of Deliverance): A concise summary of the Shifa's logic and metaphysics.
  • Al-Isharat wa-l-Tanbihat (Remarks and Admonitions): His final, deeply mature work, written in a poetic and dense style, dealing with metaphysics and mystical stations.

Legacy and European Impact

Following his death, Ibn Sina's metaphysics became the dominant framework for subsequent Islamic theology (Kalam), heavily influencing thinkers like Al-Ghazali (who criticized him but adopted his logic) and Suhrawardi. In the 12th century, his works were translated into Latin in Toledo, Spain, sparking a intellectual movement known as **Latin Avicennism**. His distinction between essence and existence was adopted by Thomas Aquinas to explain the difference between God and creation, and his works remained core curricula in European universities (such as Montpellier and Leuven) for centuries.

Primary Source Excerpts

"We say: The Necessary Existent is that which, if it is assumed not to exist, a contradiction is entailed. The contingent existent is that which, if it is assumed not to exist or to exist, no contradiction is entailed... The Necessary Existent has no cause for its existence, for if it had a cause, its existence would be through that cause. But whatever exists through something else, if left to itself, does not exist. Thus, it would be contingent, which contradicts our assumption that it is necessary."
— Ibn Sina, The Book of Deliverance (Kitab al-Najat)
"If a human being were created all at once, floating in the void, with his limbs separated so they do not touch, and his eyes covered... he would still be aware of his own existence. He would assert that he exists, even if he cannot affirm his body, his limbs, or any external matter. Thus, the soul is a distinct substance from the body, and the self is not the physical form."
— Ibn Sina, The Book of Healing (Kitab al-Shifa)
