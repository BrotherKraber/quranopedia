Shihab al-Din al-Suhrawardi (1154–1191 CE), known as Sheikh al-Ishraq ("The Master of Illumination") and often called Al-Maqtul ("The Slain") due to his execution, was a Persian philosopher and mystic who founded the Illuminationist School (Hikmat al-Ishraq) of Islamic thought. Suhrawardi launched a major critique against the Peripatetic Aristotelianism of Ibn Sina, proposing instead a metaphysics based on **Light** (Nur) and an epistemology based on intuitive self-awareness, which he termed **Knowledge by Presence**.

Biography and Tragic Execution

Suhrawardi was born in Suhraward, a village in northwest Persia. He studied jurisprudence, theology, and philosophy in Maragheh and Isfahan, mastering Avicennian logic. He lived a life of nomadic asceticism, wandering through Anatolia and Syria, meeting Sufi masters. In 1183 CE, he arrived in Aleppo, where he was warmly welcomed by the Ayyubid governor, Al-Zahir Ghazi (the son of the legendary sultan Saladin). Al-Zahir was fascinated by Suhrawardi's brilliant intellect and made him his personal tutor and advisor.

However, Suhrawardi's bold philosophical syncretism—blending Platonic metaphysics, ancient Persian light symbolism, and Sufi ecstatic utterances—along with his biting critiques of formalist scholars, drew the hostility of Aleppo's conservative legal jurists. Fearing that Suhrawardi was corrupting the young governor, the jurists petitioned Saladin, arguing that the philosopher was a threat to religious orthodoxy. Saladin, requiring the political support of the jurists to unite Muslims against the Crusaders, ordered Al-Zahir to execute Suhrawardi. He was imprisoned and died in the citadel of Aleppo in 1191 CE at the young age of 37.

The Critique of Aristotelianism

In his masterpiece, Hikmat al-Ishraq (The Philosophy of Illumination), Suhrawardi challenged the foundations of Peripatetic philosophy: * **Critique of Definition:** Aristotle argued that we define things by their genus and specific difference. Suhrawardi countered that this is circular and impossible, as we can never know all the attributes of a thing. A definition only substitutes one set of words for another, failing to capture the lived essence of the object. * **Critique of Hylomorphism:** He rejected the Aristotelian theory that physical objects are composed of primary matter and form. Instead, he argued that physical bodies are simply "barriers" (barzakh) defined by their density and lack of light.

The Metaphysics of Light (Nur)

Suhrawardi replaced the Peripatetic metaphysics of "being and existence" with an ontology of **Light** (Nur). For Suhrawardi, reality is not divided into substances and accidents; rather, all reality consists of varying degrees of light and darkness. * **The Light of Lights (Nur al-Anwar):** God is the absolute, self-luminous source of all existence. God is the Light of Lights, whose brilliance is so intense that it overflows, emanating a hierarchy of lesser lights. * **Hierarchy of Lights:** Below God are immaterial, angelic lights (incorporeal lights) and physical bodies, which are simply dark substances that block or absorb light. All created things exist and are defined solely by their proximity to and participation in the Light of Lights. He drew inspiration from the Verse of Light (Surah An-Nur 24:35): "Allah is the Light of the heavens and the earth."

Epistemology: Knowledge by Presence (al-Ilm al-Huduri)

Suhrawardi distinguished between two fundamental types of knowledge:

  1. Conceptual Knowledge (al-Ilm al-Husuli): Indirect knowledge obtained through concepts, definitions, and mental representations (used by Peripatetic sciences). This knowledge is always mediated and prone to error.
  2. Knowledge by Presence (al-Ilm al-Huduri): Direct, immediate, and unmediated knowledge. The primary example is the soul's awareness of its own self-existence. You do not know your self-existence through a logical definition or concept; you know it because your soul is present to itself as a light.

Suhrawardi argued that true philosophical wisdom (Hikmah) is only achieved when rational discursive training (logic) is completed by spiritual purification, asceticism, and mystical contemplation, allowing the soul to receive direct illumination from the celestial worlds.

Major Works

  • Hikmat al-Ishraq (The Philosophy of Illumination): His magnum opus, outlining his critique of Aristotle, his metaphysics of light, and his theory of intuitive knowledge.
  • Hayakil al-Nur (The Temples of Light): A concise treatise explaining the nature of the soul and its ascent to the light.
  • Kitab al-Talwihat (The Book of Intimations): A major work reviewing Peripatetic logic and metaphysics from an Illuminationist perspective.
  • The Red Intellect (Aql-e Surkh): A symbolic, allegorical mystical tale written in Persian.

Primary Source Excerpt

"The Light of Lights is that which is self-luminous and gives light to all other things. Its existence is too manifest to need definition, for all things are made manifest by it, and what makes other things manifest cannot itself be hidden... The soul of man is a spark of this incorporeal, celestial light. When the soul purifies itself of material desires, it becomes receptive to the direct illumination of the Light of Lights, achieving a knowledge that is present, certain, and free from doubt."
— Suhrawardi, The Philosophy of Illumination (Hikmat al-Ishraq)
