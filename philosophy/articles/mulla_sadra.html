Sadr al-Din al-Shirazi (1571–1640 CE), universally known as Mulla Sadra, was a Persian philosopher, theologian, and mystic who led the cultural and intellectual renaissance in 17th-century Safavid Persia. He synthesized the four main streams of Islamic thought—dialectical theology (Kalam), rationalist Peripatetic philosophy (Falsafa), Illuminationist intuition (Ishraq), and Sufi gnosis (Irfan)—into a single, unified system called Transcendent Theosophy (al-Hikmah al-Muta'aliyah). Mulla Sadra's metaphysics of existence marks one of the absolute peaks of classical Islamic philosophy.

Biography: From Court to Kahak

Mulla Sadra was born in Shiraz, Persia, to a wealthy and politically influential family. He traveled to Isfahan, the Safavid capital, to complete his studies under the preeminent scholars of the age, including Mir Damad (a master of Avicennian philosophy) and Baha al-Din al-Amili (a polymath jurist and mystic). Mulla Sadra's bold syntheses of rationalism and mysticism drew the hostility of conservative court theologians, who accused him of heresy.

To escape persecution, Mulla Sadra retired to Kahak, a small, isolated village near Qom, where he lived in ascetic retreat for fifteen years. During this period of isolation, he practiced spiritual purification and claimed to have received direct mystical revelations. Rehabilitated in his later years, he was invited back to Shiraz by the governor to head the newly built Khan Madrasa, where he taught and wrote his major treatises. He died in Basra in 1640 CE while returning from his seventh pilgrimage to Mecca, performed entirely on foot.

The Primacy of Existence (Asalat al-Wujud)

Prior to Mulla Sadra, Islamic philosophers debated whether essence (mahiyyah - "what" a thing is) or existence (wujud - "that" a thing is) is the primary reality. The Illuminationists, led by Suhrawardi, argued that essence is primary, while existence is merely an abstract concept in the mind. Mulla Sadra reversed this, establishing **Primacy of Existence** (Asalat al-Wujud) as the foundation of his metaphysics.

He argued that: 1. **Existence is the Sole Reality:** Only existence exists. It is the active, concrete reality that fills the cosmos. 2. **Essence is an Abstraction:** Essence is merely a mental boundary or shadow cast by the mind when it limits existence. For example, "tree" or "human" is not a real, static essence; it is simply the intellectual limit of a specific level of existence. This ontological shift resolved major problems in theology and metaphysics, changing the view of God from a "Supreme Being" to "Pure Existence" itself.

The Gradation of Existence (Tashkik al-Wujud)

If existence is the only reality, how do we explain the diversity of the world? Mulla Sadra introduced the concept of **Gradation of Existence** (Tashkik al-Wujud).

He argued that existence is a single, continuous reality, but it manifests in varying degrees of intensity, power, and perfection—similar to how light is a single reality that ranges from the blinding sun to the dimmest candle. The universe is a graded hierarchy of existence, starting from the absolute, infinite intensity of God (Pure Existence), descending through the angelic realms, human consciousness, animals, and plants, down to the weakest, most passive degree of existence, which is prime matter.

Substantial Motion (al-Harakat al-Jawhariyyah)

Classical Aristotelian philosophy held that change only occurs in accidental properties (such as an object's color, size, or position), while the underlying substance (essence) of the object remains static and unchanging. Mulla Sadra rejected this, proposing the revolutionary doctrine of **Substantial Motion** (al-Harakat al-Jawhariyyah).

He argued that the very **substance** of the universe is in constant motion, flow, and change. The physical cosmos is a dynamic process, continuously moving upward through degrees of existential intensity. * A seed does not just change its size; its very substance moves and transforms to become a tree. * The human body and soul are in substantial motion, moving from material potentiality to spiritual actuality. The soul is "material in its origin, but spiritual in its survival" (jismaniyyat al-huduth, ruhaniyyat al-baqa), emerging from physical matter but evolving through substantial motion into an immaterial, immortal spirit.

The Four Journeys (al-Asfar al-Arba'ah)

Mulla Sadra's magnum opus is Al-Asfar al-Arba'ah (The Four Journeys). It is structured as a metaphorical journey of the human soul to achieve ultimate wisdom:

  1. The Journey from Creation to God: The soul detaches itself from material desires and uses reason to ascend to the contemplation of the Divine Essence.
  2. The Journey in God with God: The soul contemplates the Divine Names and Attributes, realizing that they are the inner reality of all things.
  3. The Journey from God back to Creation: The soul returns to the physical world, but with divine awareness, viewing the world as a manifestation of God.
  4. The Journey in Creation with God: The philosopher acts as a guide in society, helping others to achieve spiritual transformation.

Major Works

  • Al-Asfar al-Arba'ah (The Four Journeys): His massive metaphysical masterpiece.
  • Al-Shawahid al-Rububiyyah (The Divine Witnesses): A concise, accessible summary of his transcendent philosophy.
  • Al-Masha'ir (The Book of Metaphysical Penetrations): A short work focusing on the primacy of existence.

Primary Source Excerpt

"Existence is the sole, concrete reality, and it is a single, graded light that manifests in diverse degrees of intensity. Essences are nothing but the intellectual limits of these gradations, having no reality of their own except as concepts in the mind... The substance of the cosmos is in constant, substantial motion, flowing continuously toward higher stages of existence and spiritual perfection, driven by a natural love for the Light of Lights."
— Mulla Sadra, The Four Journeys (Al-Asfar al-Arba'ah)
