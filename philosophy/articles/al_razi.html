Abu Bakr Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi (865–925 CE), known to the Western world as Rhazes, was a Persian polymath, physician, and philosopher. He is universally recognized as one of the greatest clinical physicians in human history, as well as one of the most original and controversial rationalist philosophers of the Islamic Golden Age. Unlike his contemporaries who synthesized Greek philosophy with theology, Al-Razi championed an independent, Platonist-inspired metaphysics and advocated for the absolute sufficiency of reason (Aql) in all areas of human life.

Biography of a Master Physician

Al-Razi was born in Rey (near modern-day Tehran, Iran). In his youth, he studied music, alchemy, mathematics, and philosophy. He did not begin his medical studies until his thirties, but his clinical genius quickly led to his appointment as the chief physician of the royal hospital in Rey, and later, the state hospital in Baghdad. He was famous for his empirical approach to medicine, keeping detailed case studies of his patients and testing treatments systematically.

He was highly compassionate, treating the poor for free and writing a self-help medical guide for laypeople, Medicine for the Poor. In his later years, Al-Razi went blind due to cataracts and refused surgery, stating that he had seen enough of the world. He returned to Rey, where he taught and wrote until his death in 925 CE.

The Five Eternals (al-Qudama al-Khamsah)

Al-Razi rejected the Aristotelian metaphysics of "substance and form" and proposed a unique Platonist cosmology based on **Five Eternal Principles**:

  1. God (The Creator): The supreme intellect who acts with wisdom and benevolence, organizing the universe out of chaos.
  2. The Soul (Universal Soul): An eternal living substance that possessed desire but lacked reason. It fell into matter out of a desire to experience physical pleasure, creating the physical universe.
  3. Absolute Matter: The primary, formless substance from which all material things are shaped.
  4. Absolute Space: The infinite void in which matter exists.
  5. Absolute Time: The continuous, independent flow of duration, which existed before the creation of the heavens and is distinct from relative time (measured by the motion of the stars).

According to Al-Razi's myth-like cosmology, God knew that the Soul would become trapped and suffer in the physical world of matter. Out of compassion, God sent **Reason** (Aql) to the soul as a divine gift. The soul must use reason and study philosophy to recognize its immaterial origin, detach itself from material desires, and return to its spiritual home. Once all souls have returned, the physical universe will dissolve back into primary matter.

Strict Rationalism and Prophetology Critique

Al-Razi was a defender of the absolute sufficiency of **Reason** (Aql). He argued that reason is God's greatest gift to humanity, capable of discovering scientific, physical, and moral truths without the need for supernatural revelation. Because of his controversial criticisms of organized religion and prophetic authority, his philosophical writings were heavily criticized by contemporary Muslim theologians and philosophers like Al-Farabi.

He believed that all human beings are born with an equal capacity for reason. Because of these views, Al-Razi faced fierce intellectual attacks from theologians and philosophers alike, who accused him of heresy. Consequently, most of his purely philosophical books were lost, and we only know of his arguments through the refutations written by his opponents, such as the Ismaili theologian Abu Hatim al-Razi.

Ethics: The Spiritual Physick (al-Tibb al-Ruhani)

Al-Razi's most famous surviving philosophical work is Al-Tibb al-Ruhani (The Spiritual Physick). In it, he applies medical principles to the treatment of the soul's moral diseases, establishing a rationalist framework for ethics.

He argued that just as the physician cures physical diseases by balancing bodily humors, the philosopher must cure psychological and moral diseases by balancing desires under the control of the intellect. He analyzes vices like anger, envy, greed, and pride as diseases of the soul caused by the override of reason by passions. He advocates for a moderate, rational life, rejecting both extreme self-indulgence and extreme asceticism, and argued that the fear of death is irrational and can be overcome through intellectual understanding.

Medical Legacy

Al-Razi's medical achievements were legendary: * **Al-Hawi (The Comprehensive Book):** A massive 23-volume medical encyclopedia that gathered all ancient Greek, Roman, and Indian medical knowledge along with his own clinical observations. It was translated into Latin in 1279 and remained a standard textbook in European medical schools for centuries. * **Smallpox and Measles:** He wrote the first scientific treatise distinguishing smallpox from measles, detailing their clinical symptoms and treatments. * **Chemical Distillation:** He was a pioneer of early chemistry, classification of minerals, and was the first to chemically produce alcohol for medical use.

Major Works

  • Al-Tibb al-Ruhani (The Spiritual Physick): His ethical masterpiece on self-purification and cognitive behavioral therapy for the soul.
  • Al-Hawi fi al-Tibb (The Comprehensive Book on Medicine): His monumental medical encyclopedia.
  • Al-Syrat al-Falsafiyyah (The Philosophical Way of Life): A short autobiographical defense of his lifestyle and philosophy.
  • Kitab al-Asrar (The Book of Secrets): A foundational work on early chemistry and alchemy.

Primary Source Excerpt

"Reason is God's greatest gift to us, and there is nothing of higher value than it... By reason, we understand all that elevates us and makes our lives good and noble. It is through reason that we have developed medicine, mathematics, astronomy, and navigation. We must not allow passions or blind imitation to override it, for reason is our guide in this world and the next."
— Al-Razi, The Spiritual Physick (Al-Tibb al-Ruhani)
