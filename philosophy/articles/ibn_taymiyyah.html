Taqi al-Din Ahmad ibn Taymiyyah (1263–1328 CE) was a Syrian theologian, jurist, and reformer who stands as one of the most intellectually formidable figures in Islamic history. As a champion of the Athari (Traditionalist) school of theology, he launched systematic, devastating critiques against Greek rationalism (Falsafa), dialectical theology (Kalam), and Sufi monist metaphysics (Wahdat al-Wujud). Far from being a simple anti-intellectual, Ibn Taymiyyah formulated a sophisticated, alternative theory of knowledge, defending the harmony of sound reason (Aql) and authentic scriptural transmission (Naql).

Biography: The Rebel Scholar

Ibn Taymiyyah was born in Harran (in modern-day Turkey). When he was six, his family fled the invading Mongol armies, traveling to Damascus with a caravan of books. He received a premium education, mastering Hanbali jurisprudence, Hadith science, mathematics, and literature. By his early twenties, he succeeded his father as a professor of Hadith and Qur'anic exegesis in Damascus. During the Mongol campaigns against the Levant, Ibn Taymiyyah personally rallied the citizens of Damascus and issued fatwas declaring military resistance against the Mongol rulers to be a religious duty, despite their formal conversion to Islam.

Because of his uncompromising theological views, which clashed with the dominant Ash'arite establishment in Egypt and Syria, Ibn Taymiyyah was repeatedly brought to trial and spent years in prison in Cairo, Alexandria, and Damascus. He spent his time in prison writing voluminous treatises refuting his opponents. He died in 1328 CE while imprisoned in the Citadel of Damascus, after his jailers confiscated his ink, pens, and paper, leaving him unable to write.

Defense of the Athari Creed (Scripturalism)

Ibn Taymiyyah advocated for a return to the methodology of the *Salaf* (the first three generations of Muslims). He argued that human reason is subordinate to divine revelation, and strongly opposed the practice of ta'wil (metaphorical interpretation) of God's attributes used by Mu'tazilites and Ash'arites.

If the Quran states that God has a "Face" or "Hand" or is "established on the Throne," Ibn Taymiyyah's position was to accept these statements literally as described, without explaining them metaphorically (ta'wil), comparing them to creation (tashbih), or asking how (bi-la kayfa). He argued that God's attributes are real but correspond to His majesty, citing **Surah Ash-Shura (42:11)**: "There is nothing like unto Him, and He is the Hearing, the Seeing." He accused both the philosophers (who denied divine attributes to preserve divine simplicity) and the theologians of rendering God an abstract, non-existent concept.

Critique of Aristotelian Logic

Ibn Taymiyyah's most original intellectual achievement was his systematic critique of Greek logic, compiled in his book Against the Logicians (Radd 'ala al-Mantiqiyyin). He was the first medieval thinker to challenge the utility of Aristotelian logic, anticipating criticisms later made by Western philosophers like Francis Bacon and John Stuart Mill.

His critique contains two main arguments:

  1. Critique of Definition: The logicians asserted that no concept can be known except through a logical definition (genus and specific difference). Ibn Taymiyyah argued that this is false. Most human concepts (like colors, sensations, or simple entities) are known directly through experience, perception, or intuition without any recourse to definitions. Furthermore, a definition is simply a description; it does not prove the existence of the object.
  2. Critique of Syllogism: The logicians claimed that new knowledge can only be obtained through a syllogism (major premise, minor premise, conclusion). Ibn Taymiyyah argued that syllogism is circular and redundant. For example, in the classic syllogism: "All men are mortal; Socrates is a man; therefore, Socrates is mortal," the major premise ("All men are mortal") already assumes the mortality of Socrates. Thus, the syllogism does not produce new knowledge. He argued: "Aristotelian logic is neither needed by the intelligent mind nor useful for the dull one."

The Harmony of Reason and Revelation (Dar' Ta'arud)

In his 10-volume masterpiece, Dar' Ta'arud al-Aql wa-l-Naql (The Refutation of the Contradiction between Reason and Revelation), Ibn Taymiyyah sought to dismantle the "universal rule" of the theologians, which stated that when reason and scripture conflict, reason must take precedence and scripture must be interpreted metaphorically.

Ibn Taymiyyah argued that **sound reason** (aql al-sarih) and **authentic scriptural transmission** (naql al-sahih) can never contradict. * If a contradiction appears to exist, it is because either the rational argument is a flawed philosophical speculation (not a true demonstrative proof), or the scriptural text is being misinterpreted or is based on an inauthentic tradition. * True reason is naturally aligned with the **Fitrah** (the innate, uncorrupted human nature created by God). Revelation does not contradict reason; it guides and perfects it.

Major Works

  • Dar' Ta'arud al-Aql wa-l-Naql (Refutation of the Contradiction between Reason and Revelation): His monumental synthesis of logic, theology, and epistemology.
  • Radd 'ala al-Mantiqiyyin (Against the Logicians): His detailed logical critique of Aristotelian methodology.
  • Minhaj al-Sunnah al-Nabawiyyah (The Pathway of the Prophetic Tradition): A massive work defending Sunni orthodoxy and outlining his political and theological views.
  • Al-Aqidah al-Wasitiyyah (The Wasit Creed): A concise exposition of the Athari creed, written for a judge from Wasit.

Primary Source Excerpt

"The logicians assert that no knowledge can be obtained except through definitions and syllogisms... But this is false. Most human knowledge, both in the sciences and in daily life, is obtained directly through perception, experience, and the innate intellect (Fitrah), without any recourse to their artificial definitions. Their syllogisms are redundant; they do not produce new knowledge, but merely reorganize what is already known. The sound intellect naturally distinguishes truth from falsehood when illuminated by the light of prophetic revelation."
— Ibn Taymiyyah, Against the Logicians (Radd 'ala al-Mantiqiyyin)
